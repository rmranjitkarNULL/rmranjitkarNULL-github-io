FishShocker9000

The FishShocker9000 is a custom embedded bioengineering system developed for UMass Chan Thyme Labs to assist biological research on zebrafish behavior and response analysis. Designed to deliver precisely timed high-voltage stimulation pulses, the system provides researchers with fine-grained control over experimental parameters while maintaining user and animal safety. The project integrates custom PCB hardware, embedded C/C++ firmware, and Python-based automation scripts into a modular platform for repeatable, high-throughput testing. Future iterations will incorporate computer vision (CV) for real-time fish detection and automated stimulus triggering, enabling closed-loop experimental control and reducing manual intervention.

Electrical Circuit Design

The FishShocker9000 circuit is built around a Teensy 4.1 microcontroller, chosen for its high-speed clock and precise timing control. The Teensy generates programmable digital control signals, which are converted into analog outputs via a digital-to-analog converter (DAC) and then amplified through a precision operational amplifier stage. To safely deliver biphasic stimulation in aqueous environments, the amplified waveform passes through H-bridges that dynamically reverse polarity between pulses—eliminating DC offset and minimizing electrolysis in the fish tank. A buck converter regulates input power, stepping down wall voltage to stable logic and drive rails. Output current is routed through stainless-steel electrode plates that transmit the stimulation pulses within the zebrafish testing chamber. The circuit emphasizes electrical isolation, safety, and reproducibility.

PCB Design and Integration

To consolidate and ruggedize the prototype, we designed a two-layer PCB in KiCad integrating all power, logic, and signal-conditioning circuitry. The layout includes pogo-pin connectors for secure, low-resistance contacts with the electrode array and mounting holes for stable enclosure placement. The board’s routing strategy isolates high-voltage and logic domains, ensuring noise immunity and operator safety. By embedding the microcontroller, H-bridge drivers, and power management on one board, the final system achieves a compact, maintainable, and easily reproducible platform for lab deployment.

Embedded Firmware

Firmware was implemented in C/C++ using the PlatformIO development environment to ensure deterministic, low-latency control over pulse generation and timing accuracy. The modular directory structure (include/, src/, lib/) supports scalability and maintainability for future features. The firmware governs DAC output sequences, pulse width modulation, and safety interlocks for high-voltage operation, while providing routines for amplitude and duration calibration. Future firmware revisions will expand to include serial communication with a vision module, enabling real-time closed-loop stimulation triggered by fish detection algorithms.

Software Testing and Validation

System validation combined Python-based automation scripts with oscilloscope measurements to evaluate pulse amplitude, timing stability, and repetition accuracy under varying conditions. Automated parameter sweeps ensured repeatable tests and rapid calibration across experimental setups. The testing framework verified microsecond-level timing precision and reliable high-voltage switching. These scripts will later serve as the foundation for the project’s computer vision module, which will employ real-time image analysis to identify zebrafish positions and trigger electrical stimuli automatically. This upgrade will transform FishShocker9000 into an intelligent, closed-loop biological instrumentation system, reducing researcher workload and improving experimental consistency.
