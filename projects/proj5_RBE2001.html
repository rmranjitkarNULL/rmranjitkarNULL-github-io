2-DoF Planar Manipulator

This project involved designing, fabricating, and testing a 2-DoF translational planar manipulator mounted on a Pololu Romi 32U4 mobile platform to autonomously swap boxes across a three-level shelf. The system consisted entirely of custom 3D-printed ABS components—including linkages, gears, and bearing mounts—modeled and iteratively refined in SolidWorks. Each component underwent finite element analysis (FEA) to verify strength and stiffness while minimizing weight, and motion simulations validated workspace reachability and parallel end-effector motion. A 20:1 worm-gear reduction provided torque for actuation, while a pantograph linkage maintained gripper parallelism. Together, these design choices yielded a precise, repeatable, and mechanically efficient manipulator.

Design and Fabrication

The manipulator was centered around a five-bar planar linkage driven by two actuators: a DC motor with a 20:1 worm-gear stage and a servo motor with a 2:1 gear reduction for slower, smoother motion. All links, mounts, and brackets were custom-modeled and 3D-printed in ABS, enabling rapid prototyping and iteration. Press-fit axles and ball bearings were used at each joint to ensure low-friction motion and precise alignment. The pantograph-style parallelogram ensured the gripper remained level throughout its workspace, simplifying grasping tasks and improving mechanical precision. The final design prioritized symmetry, manufacturability, and robustness while using accessible student fabrication tools.

Analysis & Simulation

The design process was analysis-driven from concept to implementation. Using SolidWorks Simulation, finite element analysis (FEA) verified that critical links maintained safety factors above 2.0 under load. Free-body diagrams (FBDs) and MATLAB modeling were used to calculate joint torques, optimize actuator sizing, and refine link geometry. Additional tipping and stability calculations ensured that the Romi platform remained balanced even when the arm was fully extended. Iterative refinements—such as shortening upper links and thickening bushings—improved stiffness and reduced deflection, resulting in a more stable and efficient mechanical assembly.

Control and System Integration

Although the focus of the project was on mechanical design, an integrated control system enabled synchronized actuation between the manipulator and the mobile base. The Romi microcontroller coordinated servo and DC motor actuation through an inverse-kinematics-based position control framework. Motion commands were transmitted via serial communication, allowing precise incremental control of both joint and base movement. The software architecture emphasized reliability and simplicity, enabling smooth, coordinated motion without overcomplicating the control logic. The system demonstrated the synergy between careful mechanical design and efficient embedded control.

Testing and Performance

During testing, the manipulator successfully performed all required tasks—navigating to the shelf, grasping and swapping boxes at multiple heights, and returning to its starting position. The structure remained stable and deformation-free under continuous operation, validating FEA predictions and mechanical tolerances. Measured repeatability and grasp consistency closely matched simulation results, confirming the accuracy of the design models. Despite early challenges such as servo overheating and voltage drops, iterative refinements to the power delivery and linkage design produced a reliable and fully functional prototype that met all project requirements and competition benchmarks.
